Who Was He?

From Storekeeper to Singapore Pioneer

Chia Ann Siang arrived in Singapore at sixteen with little more than ambition. He would die one of the most influential men in colonial Chinatown.

Chia Ann Siang (谢安祥), born in Malacca around 1832, was a Hokkien Chinese merchantf who journeyed to Singapore as a teenager and spent his entire adult life building one of the most remarkable private fortunes in 19th-century colonial Singapore.

Coming from a family of Straits Chinese traders — whose roots stretched back to Fujian province in China — Chia understood commerce from childhood. When he stepped off a vessel at Singapore's Telok Ayer waterfront in 1848, he entered the employ of Boustead & Company, a prestigious British trading firm. Over the next 42 years, he rose from humble storekeeper to Chief Produce Storekeeper, accumulating the capital and connections to become a towering figure in Singapore's commercial landscape.

A savvy property investor, he purchased the hill then known as Gemmill's Hill in 1873 and built his estate upon it. That hill, which today carries his name — Ann Siang Hill — along with the connecting Ann Siang Road, stands as the most visible reminder of his legacy in the heart of Chinatown.

Chapter I

Early Life & Origins

From the shores of Fujian to the hills of Malacca — and onward to Singapore

1832

The Chia Family's Chinese Roots

The story of Chia Ann Siang begins not in Singapore, nor even in Malacca, but in the coastal province of Fujian (福建), China. It was from Fujian that his grandfather made the daring decision — sometime around 1774 — to set sail southward across the South China Sea toward the Malay Peninsula.

This was a well-worn path for Hokkien Chinese merchants. Fujian's seafaring culture, combined with economic hardship and adventurous spirit, sent tens of thousands of men to Nanyang (南洋) — "the Southern Seas" — in search of fortune, trade, and new lives. Chia's grandfather was one of them, choosing to settle in Malacca, then a thriving Straits Settlement port under Dutch and later British rule.

In Malacca, the Chia family became firmly established in the Straits Chinese (Peranakan) community — a distinct cultural group of Chinese who had lived in the Straits Settlements for generations, blending Chinese tradition with local Malay and European influences. Their identity was bound in equal measure to their Hokkien heritage and their adopted homeland.

🌊
The Great Migration to Nanyang

Throughout the 18th and 19th centuries, millions of Chinese emigrants — particularly from Fujian and Guangdong provinces — made the perilous sea journey to Southeast Asia. They came seeking trade, work, and opportunity. These migrants, known as the "Nanyang Chinese" (南洋华人), would become the backbone of commerce in port cities like Penang, Malacca, and Singapore.

👴
Father: Chia Poh Eng

Chia Ann Siang's father, Chia Poh Eng, was himself a respected merchant in Malacca. He was notable enough to be mentioned by the celebrated Malay writer Abdullah Munshi in his memoir Hikayat Pelayaran — a testament to the family's standing in the Malaccan mercantile world. Chia Ann Siang was one of many sons born into this prominent household.

✦
The Journey

Arriving in Singapore

In 1848, a sixteen-year-old boy from Malacca stepped onto Singapore's shores — and changed his destiny.

In 1848, at just sixteen years of age, Chia Ann Siang made the short but significant journey from Malacca to Singapore. It was a move that would define the rest of his life. Singapore, founded by Sir Stamford Raffles in 1819, was barely three decades old yet already exploding as one of the most important trading ports in Asia.

The Singapore that young Chia entered was a city of extraordinary energy and opportunity. The Telok Ayer waterfront — near where he would one day buy his first home — was alive with the sounds of ships unloading goods from China, India, Europe, and the Malay Archipelago. The Chinese community, particularly Hokkien and Teochew immigrants, dominated much of the commercial activity along the Singapore River and in Chinatown.

Chia was not arriving as a labourer or a "coolie." His family background gave him an introduction: he secured employment with Boustead & Company, one of the most important British trading firms in the Straits Settlements. This would prove to be the foundation upon which his entire future was built.

Singapore in 1848: A young port city of approximately 60,000 people, bustling with immigrants from China, India, and the Malay Archipelago. The Chinese community — concentrated in Chinatown — was the largest single group, and Hokkien merchants held particular sway in the spice, tin, and textile trades.

The Straits Chinese Identity: As a Malacca-born Peranakan, Chia Ann Siang occupied a privileged middle ground — familiar with Western colonial business practices (through Malacca's long European history) yet deeply rooted in Chinese culture and language. This made him a natural bridge between British trading firms and the local Chinese community.

"Chia Ann Siang was born in Malacca in 1832, being one of a large family of sons of Chia Poh Eng, a native of Malacca who was mentioned by Abdullah Munshi in his Hikayat Pelayaran and who was one of the leading merchants of his time."

Chapter II

Career & Commerce

Forty-two years of diligence at the heart of Singapore's colonial trade economy

1848

Boustead & Company

When Chia Ann Siang joined Boustead & Company in 1848, he was entering one of the most influential British trading houses in the Straits Settlements. Founded by Edward Boustead, the firm was a quintessential example of 19th-century colonial commerce — trading in the natural resources that fuelled both Asian and European markets.

Chia began as a storekeeper, responsible for receiving, cataloguing, and dispatching the enormous volumes of goods that flowed through Boustead's warehouses. It was unglamorous work, but it placed him at the crossroads of Singapore's trade economy — and he used every moment to observe, learn, and build relationships.

His dedication was recognised swiftly. After just eight years on the job, he was promoted to the senior position of Chief Produce Storekeeper — a remarkable rise for a young immigrant from Malacca. He would hold this position and remain with the firm for a total of 42 years before retiring in 1890.

⚓
What Boustead Traded

Chia Ann Siang handled goods that connected the world's great markets. Boustead's ships plied the China–Europe routes, driven by insatiable demand on both ends:

Spices 香料 Coconut 椰子 Tobacco 烟草 Tin 锡 Tea 茶叶 Silk 丝绸 Natural Resources Textiles

Boustead's ships actively plied the China–Europe trade routes throughout the early 19th century, making Singapore a critical waystation in one of the world's most lucrative commercial corridors.

✦

A Career in Three Acts

📦
Act I: The Storekeeper (1848)

At 16, Chia joins Boustead as a junior storekeeper. He manages the flow of goods through the firm's Singapore warehouses — spices from the Archipelago, tin from the Malay Peninsula, silk from China. Every shipment teaches him about value, supply, and the rhythms of global trade.

🏅
Act II: Chief Storekeeper (1856)

After eight years of exemplary service, Chia is promoted to Chief Produce Storekeeper. He now oversees entire cargo operations, building deep trust with Boustead's British principals and accumulating capital from his salary and trading commissions. He begins investing in property.

🤝
Act III: Partnership (1863)

Chia diversifies. From 1863 onwards he is a partner of Geok Teat & Company, a Chinese commercial enterprise, expanding his business footprint beyond Boustead while simultaneously deepening his roots in Singapore's Chinese commercial network.

"During his long connection with that firm extending over a period of forty-two years, he gradually built up a fortune, so that at his death in 1892 he was a wealthy man and landowner on a large scale."

🌏
Geok Teat & Company (1863 onwards)

Beyond his work at Boustead, Chia's partnership in Geok Teat & Company from 1863 placed him firmly within the Chinese merchant class. This dual commercial identity — trusted by the British at Boustead while respected by Chinese business networks — was a rare and powerful position in colonial Singapore.

Such partnerships were the lifeblood of Singapore's economy. Chinese trading houses and clan networks provided credit, trust, and market intelligence across the entire Southeast Asian region. Chia's membership in this world accelerated his wealth accumulation considerably.

🎖️
Retirement After 42 Years (1890)

In 1890, after an extraordinary 42 years of service, Chia Ann Siang retired from Boustead & Company. He was by then already wealthy beyond measure — a landowner with properties across Chinatown, a man whose name was associated with one of Singapore's most prominent hills.

His retirement was short-lived. He died just two years later, on 23 September 1892, at the age of approximately 60. But the fortune he left behind would sustain his family for two full generations and fund the education of a third.

Chapter III

The Property Empire

How a storekeeper's wages became an empire of brick, hill, and heritage

1873

Chia Ann Siang was not merely a merchant — he was a visionary property investor who understood that in a booming port city, land was the ultimate asset. From his early purchases along Telok Ayer Street to the acquisition of an entire hill in the heart of Chinatown, his property portfolio told the story of Singapore's rapid colonial expansion.

✦
First Acquisition
58 Telok Ayer Street

Chia's first significant property purchase was a house at 58 Telok Ayer Street. He acquired this within the first six years of his career at Boustead — a remarkable feat of thrift and ambition for a young immigrant.

Telok Ayer Street was the soul of Hokkien Singapore. Stretching along what was once the seafront (before land reclamation), it was lined with temples, clan associations, remittance houses, and merchant homes. To own property here was to be at the centre of Chinese commercial and cultural life.

Second Acquisition
Amoy Street House

Not content with one property, Chia also acquired a house along Amoy Street — another key thoroughfare in Singapore's Chinese quarter.

Amoy Street (named after Amoy, or Xiamen in Fujian province, the ancestral homeland of many Hokkien immigrants) was home to some of the most important Chinese businesses and institutions of the era, including the Cuiying Shuyuan school to which Chia would later donate. Owning property here signalled his growing status.

The Centrepiece: Ann Siang Hill (1873)

In 1873, Chia Ann Siang purchased a large piece of land from John Gemmill — a transaction that would immortalise his name in Singapore's geography forever.

The Hill's History Before Chia

The hill had a layered past before Chia's purchase. Originally known as Scott's Hill, it was owned by Charles Scott, who cultivated a nutmeg and clove estate upon its slopes — a common enterprise in early colonial Singapore, where spice cultivation was a major industry.

Scott later sold the hill to John Gemmill, who renamed it Gemmill's Hill. A Cantonese burial ground — one of Singapore's oldest Chinese cemeteries — occupied the foot of the hill, where it met Mount Erskine, and remained in use until 1867.

When Chia purchased the hill from Gemmill in 1873, he built his house and private estate upon it. The hill was thereafter known as Ann Siang Hill in his honour — a name that persists to this day in Singapore's official maps and street signs.

🗻
Mount Erskine

Beyond Ann Siang Hill, Chia also acquired Mount Erskine — another significant piece of land in the Chinatown/Tanjong Pagar area. This acquisition further cemented his status as one of Singapore's pre-eminent landowners of the 19th century.

The foot of Mount Erskine was where the ancient Cantonese burial ground had been located before its exhumation in 1907. Chia's ownership of land in this area demonstrated his willingness to invest in what was, at the time, the periphery of the established urban core — a prescient decision.

🏙️
70+ Properties in Chinatown

At the time of his death in 1892, and as managed by his estate in subsequent decades, Chia's property holdings in Chinatown numbered over 70 houses. By 1919, this extraordinary portfolio — maintained and possibly expanded by his descendants — remained intact.

This concentration of property in Singapore's most densely populated Chinese district made the Chia estate one of the largest private landlords in colonial Singapore. The income from these properties supported his family for generations.

By 1919: The Chia estate held more than 70 houses in Chinatown alone.

"An astute property investor, Chia continued to acquire much real estate in the course of his life; by 1919, he owned more than 70 houses in Chinatown."

Chapter IV

Giving Back — Philanthropy

Education, culture, and community: Chia's generosity left marks as lasting as his name on a hill

❤️

Chia Ann Siang understood what it meant to arrive in a new land with few resources. As his wealth grew, so did his commitment to supporting the institutions that sustained Singapore's Chinese immigrant community. His philanthropic contributions allowed the development of education, culture, and the welfare of the dead and living alike.

In 19th-century Singapore, private philanthropy by wealthy merchants was a cornerstone of community development. There was no state welfare, no public education system for the Chinese — it fell to men of means to fund the schools, temples, and civic institutions that served thousands of immigrants. Chia Ann Siang was among those men.

✦
1
Chongwen Ge — The Literary Pavilion
崇文阁 — Telok Ayer Street, next to Thian Hock Keng Temple

Chia Ann Siang donated to the building fund of Chongwen Ge (崇文阁), a literary and cultural pavilion located next to the revered Thian Hock Keng Temple (天福宫) on Telok Ayer Street — one of Singapore's most important Hokkien sites.

The name Chongwen Ge means "Pavilion for Honouring Literature" — a reflection of the deep Confucian value placed on education and the written word. Such institutions provided a cultural anchor for the immigrant community, preserving Chinese language, arts, and scholarship in a foreign land.

Thian Hock Keng Temple itself — the most important Hokkien temple in Singapore, built between 1839 and 1842 — was gazetted as a National Monument in 1973. Chia's connection to institutions around this sacred site reflects his deep roots in the Hokkien community and his dedication to preserving its cultural heritage.

Cultural Education Hokkien Community Literature & Arts Telok Ayer
2
Cuiying Shuyuan — The Academy at Amoy Street
萃英书院 — Amoy Street (formerly known as Chui Eng Institute)

Chia Ann Siang also donated to the building fund of Cuiying Shuyuan (萃英书院) at Amoy Street — known in English as the Chui Eng Institute or Cui Ying Academy. The name translates roughly as "Academy for Gathering Excellence."

Originally taught in Hokkien, this school was considered the top Chinese school in Singapore of its era. It served the sons of merchants and immigrants who might otherwise have had no access to education in their mother tongue. To donate to its construction was to invest directly in the future of Singapore's Chinese community.

It is worth noting that another great Chinese philanthropist of the era, Tan Kim Seng, also donated generously to this institution — placing Chia Ann Siang in the company of Singapore's most celebrated Victorian-era benefactors.

3
The Gift of Land — Hokkien Cemetery & Public Burial Ground
福建义山 — Bukit Timah Road

Perhaps the most poignant of Chia's philanthropic acts was his donation of land for a Hokkien cemetery on Bukit Timah Road. This private burial ground, which adjoined the established Hokkien cemetery, was gifted by Chia to the Municipality for use as a public burial ground.

For Chinese immigrants in the 19th century, a proper burial was a matter of immense cultural and spiritual significance. To die far from one's ancestral homeland without a proper resting place was a source of profound anxiety. By donating land for burials, Chia offered comfort — and dignity — to an entire community.

The irony is that Chia himself would eventually be buried in this ground — and his grave would be lost and forgotten for over a century, only rediscovered in 2012 with the help of heritage volunteers and descendants.

"He was also a generous philanthropist and donated to the building funds of institutions such as Chongwen Ge (崇文阁) next to the Thian Hock Keng Temple (天福宫) at Telok Ayer Street, and Cuiying Shuyuan (萃英书院) at Amoy Street."

The Philanthropist's Creed

In donating to education, culture, and burial welfare, Chia Ann Siang embodied a uniquely Chinese-Singaporean model of civic responsibility: the wealthy merchant as community father — giving back to the community that had made his success possible.

Chapter V

Family & Children

Eight generations of a Straits Chinese family, from Fujian to present-day Singapore

👨‍👩‍👧

Marriage to Chan Chee Lee Neo

Chia Ann Siang was married to Chan Chee Lee Neo, a Peranakan woman of the Straits Chinese community. Their union was typical of the era's merchant class: alliances between prominent families reinforced through marriage, deepening both social bonds and business networks.

Together, they raised a family that would carry his legacy forward — through triumph and through controversy. Chia was no passive patriarch: he was deeply involved in his children's lives and marriages, to the point where he took the extraordinary step of disinheriting one son for making a marriage against his wishes.

Chia Chee Bee Neo — The Union That Joined Two Empires

Chia's daughter Chia Chee Bee Neo (born c. 1860) made arguably the most significant marriage in the family's history. Her union brought together two of Singapore's wealthiest families — a strategic alliance that consolidated economic and social power across Chinese merchant circles in the late 19th century.

Such marriages were a key mechanism by which Peranakan merchant families grew their influence. By marrying into another prominent family, Chee Bee Neo ensured that the Chia family's wealth, networks, and prestige would be magnified rather than diminished across generations.

⚖️
The Disinherited Son — A Father's Rigid Will

The most dramatic episode in the Chia family story is the disinheritance of one son who dared to marry against his father's wishes. In a society where marriage was as much a business transaction as a personal matter, Chia Ann Siang's refusal to accept his son's choice was an assertion of patriarchal authority.

The consequences were stark: the son was formally cut from the inheritance, his branch of the family effectively separated from the main line of Chia wealth. This story, preserved in family memory and documented in the book Chia Ann Siang and Family: The Tides of Fortune (2019), speaks to both the power and the rigid expectations that came with being a 19th-century patriarch.

"Chia Ann Siang died in 1892, leaving a fortune that supported two generations of his family and educated a third. This book looks at the disparate fortunes of his descendants, including a son he disinherited for marrying against his wishes, and a daughter whose marriage brought together two of Singapore's wealthiest families."

Epigram Books — Chia Ann Siang and Family: The Tides of Fortune (2019), by Ruth Chia, Linda Kow & Soh Tiang Keng
🪦
The Grave Discovery of 2012 — Descendants Reunited

For over a century, the precise location of Chia Ann Siang's grave in the Bukit Timah area was unknown, overgrown, and forgotten — a melancholy irony for a man who had donated land for the very cemetery in which he lay.

In September 2012, heritage volunteers from the Bukit Brown Heritage Group — led by a researcher named Raymond Goh — located the grave, which had been neglected for more than 100 years. The discovery prompted an emotional reunion of his great-great-grandchildren.

"This is indeed a very special day! Many thanks to Raymond Goh from Bukit Brown Heritage group for locating the grave of my great, great grandfather Chia Ann Siang. It was neglected and lost for over a 100 years. He was a prominent land owner in early Singapore who died on 23 Sep 1892. Today I met my cousins, Anthony Sng, Raphael, Alan & Alphonsus, all fellow great, great grandsons of Chia Ann Siang."

Alvin Lee (Great-great-grandson of Chia Ann Siang) — Bukit Brown blog, September 2012
Chapter VI

A Living Legacy

Ann Siang Hill stands today as a testament to the immigrant who built upon it

今
🪧
Ann Siang Hill & Ann Siang Road
The most tangible legacy: two streets in Singapore's heart bear his name to this day. Ann Siang Hill links Club Street and Ann Siang Road to South Bridge Road. Ann Siang Road connects Ann Siang Hill to Kadayanallur Street. Lined with elegantly restored shophouses built between 1903 and 1941, the area is one of Singapore's most beloved heritage precincts.
🏛️
Clan Associations & Social Clubs
Ann Siang Road was once the traditional home of Chinese clan associations and exclusive social clubs. These organisations — pivotal to the welfare of new immigrants — found their natural home on the hill that bore Chia's name. The area represented the organised social infrastructure of Chinese Singapore.
✉️
The Remittance Hub
Ann Siang Hill was the traditional site of remittance houses, where early Chinese immigrants sent money back to their families in China. Letter writers and calligraphers operated from the five-foot ways, helping illiterate immigrants write letters home. Chia's hill was at the emotional centre of the migrant experience.
🌿
Ann Siang Hill Park & Pioneers Trail
Today, Ann Siang Hill Park — launched by the National Heritage Board in collaboration with the National Parks Board — features a 0.6 km self-guided Pioneers Trail through the park and Telok Ayer Green. The trail traces pre-colonial roots, spice trade influences, and the roles of historic shophouses in Chinese clan associations.
🏨
Ann Siang House (2018)
Opened in 2018, Ann Siang House is a boutique hotel spanning restored shophouses at 28 Ann Siang Road. It exemplifies adaptive reuse of heritage architecture: original facades preserved while modern luxury amenities are integrated within the conservation district — a living example of Chia's property vision enduring into the 21st century.
📚
The Book: Tides of Fortune (2019)
In 2019, Epigram Books published Chia Ann Siang and Family: The Tides of Fortune by Ruth Chia, Linda Kow, and Soh Tiang Keng. The most detailed portrait of his life ever written, it traces eight generations of the Chia clan from Fujian to present-day Singapore. This scholarship ensures his story will not be forgotten.
✦

"Ann Siang Hill serves as a key heritage symbol in contemporary Singapore, embodying Chinatown's transformation from a 19th-century immigrant enclave to a meticulously preserved cultural district that highlights the nation's layered history and urban renewal efforts."

The Shophouses of Ann Siang (1903–1941)

Most of the shophouses that define Ann Siang Hill and Ann Siang Road today were built between 1903 and 1941. These two- and three-storey structures, with their characteristic five-foot ways, louvred shutters, and ornate plasterwork, represent the architectural legacy of Singapore's Chinese immigrant community at its height.

The conservation of these shophouses — many now occupied by restaurants, bars, boutique hotels, and creative businesses — has made Ann Siang Hill one of Singapore's most photographed and visited heritage precincts.

An Immigrant Who Shaped a Nation

Chia Ann Siang arrived in Singapore as a sixteen-year-old storekeeper's apprentice. He left it 44 years later as one of its most consequential private citizens — a man whose name is literally embedded in the map of the city, whose charitable works built schools and cultural institutions, and whose property empire shaped the development of Chinatown for a century after his death.

His story is Singapore's story: the story of migration, ambition, community, and belonging — of a man who came from elsewhere and made this island his home, forever.

1 National Library Board (NLB) — Chia Ann Siang Reference Guide
National Library Board Singapore  |  Singapore Government Institution  |  Last updated: 19 May 2026

The primary NLB reference guide on Chia Ann Siang provides a concise official biography and bibliography of books, articles, and newspaper sources about his life. It is the cornerstone reference for all biographical information on this site.

"Chia Ann Siang (谢安祥; 1832–1892) was a Hokkien merchant born in Malacca. In 1848, when he was 16, Chia was hired by British firm Boustead and Company who traded in natural resources such as spices, coconut, tobacco, tin, tea and silk."
"He was also a generous philanthropist and donated to the building funds of institutions such as Chongwen Ge (崇文阁) next to the Thian Hock Keng Temple (天福宫) at Telok Ayer Street, and Cuiying Shuyuan (萃英书院) at Amoy Street."
→ reference.nlb.gov.sg/guides/singapore/people/chia-ann-siang/
2 NLB Infopedia — "Ann Siang Road" by Thulaja, Naidu Ratnala (2016)
Singapore Infopedia, National Library Board  |  Published 2016

The authoritative NLB Infopedia article on Ann Siang Road details the history of the street, the hill, and the various owners of the land before and after Chia's tenure. It provides architectural and urban history context.

"The hill became known as Ann Siang Hill after it came to be owned by Chia Ann Siang who built his house and estate on the site."
"Ann Siang Road in Chinatown begins from a road known as Ann Siang Hill and connects to Kadayanallur Street. It is named after Chia Ann Siang (1832–1892) who was a wealthy Chinese businessman."
→ nlb.gov.sg — Ann Siang Road, Infopedia
3 Epigram Books — Chia Ann Siang and Family: The Tides of Fortune (2019)
Authors: Ruth Chia, Linda Kow, Soh Tiang Keng  |  Publisher: Epigram Books, Singapore  |  ISBN: 9789814868303

The most comprehensive biographical account of Chia Ann Siang, tracing eight generations of the Chia clan from Fujian to present-day Singapore. Essential for information on his children, descendants, and family dynamics.

"Born in Malacca in 1834, Chia joined Boustead and Co., an English trading company in Singapore, as a storekeeper at the age of 16. In just six years, he was made Chief Storekeeper, and was able to buy himself a house at 58 Telok Ayer Street (and another one on Amoy Street for good measure)."
"Chia Ann Siang died in 1892, leaving a fortune that supported two generations of his family and educated a third. This book looks at the disparate fortunes of his descendants, including a son he disinherited for marrying against his wishes, and a daughter whose marriage brought together two of Singapore's wealthiest families."
→ epigrambookshop.sg — Tides of Fortune
4 Geni.com — Genealogy Profile for Chia Ann Siang 谢安祥
Geni.com  |  Collaborative genealogy record  |  Last updated: September 2012

The Geni genealogy profile for Chia Ann Siang contains a detailed biographical extract based on colonial-era documentation, recording his family tree including wife Chan Chee Lee Neo and children Chia Chee Hay Neo, Chia Chee Bee Neo (b. 1860), and Chia Keng Beng (b. 1864).

"Chia Ann Siang entered the firm of Boustead & Co. at the age of 16, and after eight years' service was promoted to be chief produce storekeeper. During his long connection with that firm extending over a period of forty-two years, he gradually built up a fortune, so that at his death in 1892 he was a wealthy man and landowner on a large scale."
"He was buried in his private burial ground adjoining the Hokkien cemetery in Bukit Timah Road, the land for which had been given by him to the Municipality for the purposes of a public burial ground."
→ geni.com — Chia Ann Siang Genealogy Profile
5 Bukit Brown Heritage Blog — "A Family Reunion (Chia Ann Siang Part II)"
bukitbrown.com  |  Published: 23 September 2012  |  Author: Catherine Lim / Bukit Brown Heritage Group

A first-person account of the 2012 rediscovery of Chia Ann Siang's grave and the reunion of his descendants. Contains direct quotes from great-great-grandson Alvin Lee.

"This is indeed a very special day! Many thanks to Raymond Goh from Bukit Brown Heritage group for locating the grave of my great, great grandfather Chia Ann Siang. It was neglected and lost for over a 100 years. He was a prominent land owner in early Singapore who died on 23 Sep 1892. Today I met my cousins, Anthony Sng, Raphael, Alan & Alphonsus, all fellow great, great grandsons of Chia Ann Siang." — Alvin Lee, great-great-grandson
→ bukitbrown.com — A Family Reunion
6 Wikipedia — "Ann Siang Hill"
Wikipedia  |  Free Encyclopedia  |  Continuously updated

The Wikipedia article on Ann Siang Hill provides context on the area's remittance house history, the Chinese community's use of the hill, and the conservation of its shophouses. It draws on academic and institutional sources.

"The early Chinese immigrants visited Ann Siang Hill when they wanted to send money home to their families in China, as it was the traditional site of remittance houses. Letter writers and calligraphers also had their businesses at the five-foot way of the shophouses to help the illiterate immigrants write letters home."
→ en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ann_Siang_Hill
